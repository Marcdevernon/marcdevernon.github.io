I write up my understanding of the LOD method presented in Peterseim and Malqvist's book, "Numerical Homogenization by Localized Orthogonal Decomposition" published by SIAM in 2021.

Chapter 2
======

Chapter 2.1
----

The authors provide an initial introduction to why homogenization is necessary for problems with fine-scale and coarse-scale spatial discretization. They do this by considering a one-dimensional diffusion problem on $\Omega :=(0,1)$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(A_{\epsilon}(x)\frac{d}{dx}u_{\epsilon}(x)) = f(x) in \Omega,\\u_{\epsilon}(1)=0$$

$u_{\epsilon}$ is positive, $\epsilon>0$ is a small number. The unique solution, $u_{\epsilon}$ is located in the Sobolev space $V:=H_{0}^{1}$. It has a variational formulation: 

$$
\int_{\Omega} A_{\epsilon}u_{\epsilon}'v' \, dx = \int_{\Omega} fv \, dx\ for\ all\ v \in V.
$$

Here the forcing is $f(x):= 1$ and $A_{\epsilon}(x) := (2+cos(2\pi\frac{x}{\epsilon}))^{-1}$.
Note that $\epsilon^{-1}\in\mathbb{N}$

The unique solution is 
$$u_{\epsilon}=x-x^{2}+\frac{\epsilon}{2\pi}(\frac{1}{2}-x)sin(2\pi\frac{x}{\epsilon}) + \frac{\epsilon^{2}}{4\pi^{2}}(1-cos(2\pi\frac{x}{\epsilon})$$.

The Galerkin finite element method finds the best aproximation in a finite-dimensional subspace $V_h \subset V$ Such that:

$$V_h = \{\omega\in\mathbb{C}^0(\overline{\Omega})|\forall T\in\mathbb{T}_h:\omega|_T\ is\ affine\ and\ \omega(0)=\omega(1)=0\}.$$

A standard choice is conforming first order-finite elements $P_1-FEM$ on a uniform mesh 

$$\mathcal{T}_h:=\{[(j-1)h,jh]\ |j=1,...,h^{-1}\}$$

defined on the unit interval with mesh size $0<h<1$.

For various sizes of $h$ and $\epsilon=2^{-5}$ this looks like the following. Note that the aproximiation is terrible when $h>\epsilon$
![Finite Element Aproximation for $u_{\epsilon}$ for different sizes of $h$.](http://localhost:4000/images/fig2.2 - LOD.jpeg)

To understand this behavior a little better, note that the definition of the mean of a lebesque integrable function is:

$$\unicode{x2A0F}_T v dx = \frac{1}{|T|} \int_T v dx$$

With this, we can look at the bilinear form defined on the mesh:

$$
\int_{\Omega} A_{\epsilon,h}u_{\epsilon,h}'v_{h}' \, dx = \sum_{T\in\mathbb{T}_h}\int_T(\unicode{x2A0F}_T A_{\epsilon} dx)u_{\epsilon,h}'v_{h}' \, dx = \int_{\Omega}\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}u_{\epsilon,h}'v_{h}'\, dx $$

The authors consider the mean value as a single point quadrature using a small enough mesh. (They used the fact that derivatives on P1-FEM meshes are constant - Tent functions have constant derivatives, and the periodicity of $A_{\epsilon}$). Since P1-FEM is exact on the nodes of the mesh in the case of constant diffusion, FE solutions are nodal interpolants of:

$$u(x)=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}(x-x^2)$$

This solves the PDE:

$$\frac{-1}{\sqrt{3}}u''=1$$

This amplitude is larger than the amplitude of $u_{\epsilon}$ by $\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$. Also note that once $h<\epsilon$, the mean values of $$\unicode{x2A0F}_T A_\epsilon(s) ds$$ start to approach $A_{\epsilon}$, causing the method to switch to its expected asymptotic behavior.

Other FEM methods suffer from the same basic problem when there are finely fluctuating features. Thus we need a better approach.
